Neutron Star · PSR J0534+2200

The Crab Pulsar

The crushed, spinning core of a star that a supernova ripped apart — an event ancient astronomers watched and recorded in the year 1054.

The Real Object

A lighthouse 6,500 light-years away

When a massive star runs out of fuel and collapses, its core can crush down into a neutron star — an object so dense that a teaspoon of it would weigh billions of tons. As it collapses, it spins up dramatically and sweeps a beam of radio energy around like a lighthouse. Every time that beam crosses Earth, we detect a pulse — for the Crab Pulsar, that happens roughly 30 times every single second.

Why it sounds this way

real spin frequency → repeating click rate

Rather than a smooth tone, I generated sharp clicks at exactly the pulsar's real measured spin frequency — 29.95 times per second. That rate is fast enough for the human ear to perceive as a continuous buzzing texture rather than individual clicks, which is genuinely how this pulsar would sound if directly sonified at real speed — no artistic exaggeration needed.
