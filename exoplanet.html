Kepler-10 b

One of the first confirmed rocky planets beyond our solar system — orbiting so close to its star that a year there lasts less than a day here.

The Real Object

A world we've never seen — only measured

Kepler-10 b was one of the first small, rocky exoplanets confirmed by NASA's Kepler mission. It orbits its star in just under 20 hours, so close that its surface is likely a permanent ocean of molten rock. We can't photograph it directly — instead, astronomers detect it by watching its star's light dim very slightly every time the planet passes in front of it, a "transit."

The Mapping

Why it sounds this way

real orbital period → repeating volume dip

I generated a steady tone representing the star's constant light, then used the planet's real measured orbital period and transit duration to briefly dim that tone's volume at the correct, repeating interval — one dip per orbit. The real transit only dims the star by 0.02%, far too subtle to hear, so I exaggerated the dip's size for audibility while keeping its timing scientifically accurate.
