Bonus Collection

More Sounds of the Universe

A lighter, more playful set of sonifications — each still grounded in one real fact, but explored quickly rather than in full depth.

Unlike the four core objects, these are quick explorations — each based on one real number, without the same depth of data cleaning, scale design, or written reasoning as the main collection. Consider them sketches, not finished pieces.
Gas Giant

Jupiter

The fastest-spinning planet in our solar system — one full rotation in just 9.9 hours. Sonified as a fast, low oscillation matching that real spin rate, compressed to an audible range.

Ringed Planet

Saturn

Saturn's day is 10.7 hours, and it takes 29.5 Earth years to orbit the sun. Two layered tones represent its fast spin and its slow, distant orbit at once.

Planet

Mars

Mars's day is almost exactly Earth-length — 24.6 hours — making it one of the most "familiar-feeling" rotation rates in the solar system. Sonified as a steady, Earth-like pulse.

Galaxy

Andromeda (M31)

Our nearest large galactic neighbor, 2.5 million light-years away — and it's actually moving toward us, at about 110 km/s, destined to merge with the Milky Way in ~4.5 billion years. Sonified as a slowly descending drone, representing that inbound motion.

Comet

Comet NEOWISE

A comet discovered in 2020 with a much shorter, less extreme orbit than Halley (~6,800 years!). A quick brightness-to-pitch sketch, same idea as Halley but far less refined.

Gravitational Waves

Black Hole Merger (GW150914)

The first gravitational wave ever directly detected, in 2015 — two black holes spiraling together and merging, chirping upward in frequency right before colliding. This is a simplified recreation of that real, famous "chirp."

These sketches use real reference numbers (rotation/orbital periods, recession velocity, the GW150914 chirp) but are simplified for quick exploration.
