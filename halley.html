Halley's Comet

A 76-year journey from the freezing dark past Neptune to a brief, brilliant swing around the sun — and back out again.

The Real Object

A journey too long to observe directly

Halley's Comet has been recorded by human observers for over 2,000 years, and its orbit was the first ever mathematically predicted, by Edmond Halley in 1705. Its orbit is extremely elongated — spending most of its 76-year cycle far beyond Neptune, invisibly faint, before swinging in past Venus's orbit and blazing brightly for a brief stretch. It was last near the sun in 1986, and won't return until 2061.

The Mapping

Why it sounds this way

calculated brightness (log-scaled) → pitch on a Lydian scale · orbital speed → tempo

No dataset covers Halley's full 76-year orbit directly — it's too faint to observe for most of that time. Instead, I used its real orbital elements and solved Kepler's Equation numerically to calculate its distance and speed from the sun at any point in the cycle. Because a comet's brightness changes so extremely near the sun, a direct mapping produced near-silence with one sharp spike — so I applied a logarithmic scale, the same technique real astronomical magnitude measurements use for the same reason. Pitch follows brightness on a Lydian scale (for an open, dreamlike quality), while note length follows the comet's real orbital speed — faster and shorter near the sun, slower and longer in deep space.
